ARCH - Forecasting Volatility in Financial Time Series

Understanding the intuition behind the ARCH models

Jan 29, 2021 • 3 min read

  • ARCH?

ARIMA models are the most used models when forecasting time series. I've explained ARIMA in my last post, but to explain it simply — ARIMA is combination of twp different models Auto Regressive(AR) and Moving Average(MA). The AR part models the predicted value on past values of itself where as the MA part models the predicted value on past values of the residuals. So, one can forecast the futiure price of the stock using ARIMA models.

What if instead of forecasting the price of a stock, you need to know — When is it the best time to buy a stock? or in other words — When is it not very risky to invest in a stock?

ARIMA models assumes constant variance in the data. But in real financial markets, the data has non-constant variance over time. In financial domain, this variance is called as volatility. This property of increasing or decreasing volatility is called as heteroskedasticity. How to model this change in variance?

ARCH is a method that explicitly models this change in variance over time in financial time series. There are many extensions to ARCH, such as GARCH, ARCH-M, GARCH-M, TGARCH, EGARCH and few others. Out of all these models, GARCH is extensively used in financial markets — we'll also look into this after underastnding ARCH.

ARCH?

What is ARCH?

ARCH stands for Auto Regressive Conditional Heteroskedasticity.

It is very similar to ARMA model, but the CH part models the past values of squared residuals instead of residuals. Let's look into this in detail —

  • Auto Regressive - The observation at current time period can be expressed as a function of observations at previous time periods
  • Heteroskedasticity - The data displays varying variance
  • Conditional - The varying variance is time dependent

The basic idea behind ARCH model is the variance of error at a time period is correlated with the variance of errors at its lagged time periods.

ARCH(1) model is given by the following equation:

var(e(t)) = σ²(t) = ⍺0 + ⍺1 * σ²(t-1)

The actual series of residuals is given as:

e(t) = w(t) * σ(t) ⇒ w(t) * sqrt(⍺0 + ⍺1 * (e(t-1))²)

where:

  • e(t) is volatility of the error at t
  • w(t) is white noise

So ARCH model captures the correlation between the squared residulas which is nothing but the correlation between the variance at different time lags.

Now let's simulate a time series with ARCH model to verify this. We will look into a series which is simulated by ARCH(1).

Simulating and verifying ARCH(1) process 

np.random.seed(13)

n = 1000

alpha_0 = 0.2
alpha_1 = 0.4

series = [gauss(0,1), gauss(0,1)]
vols = [1, 1]

for _ in range(n):
    new_vol = np.sqrt(alpha_0 + alpha_1*series[-1]**2)
    new_val = gauss(0,1) * new_vol
    vols.append(new_vol)
    series.append(new_val)

Now, lets plot the PACF plot of the series:

plot_pacf(np.array(series))
plt.show()

Notice that the series just looks like a white noise with no correlations. But, lets see what happens when we plot the PACF plot for the same series but with the squared values.

plot_pacf(np.array(series)**2)
plt.show()

Notice that the PACF is showing significance at lag 1 which indicates AR(1) model might be an appropriate method to model the squared values.

Now lets see how well this model forecasts the volatility in the series. Remember that we simulated the data to work well for ARCH(1) model. Here, we are going in backward direction to understand the ARCH model i.e. we first decided the model parameters and then simulated the data.

Divide the simulated series into train and test sets and fit the ARCH(1) model. We can use arch package in python for fitting the models.

test_size = int(n*0.1)
rolling_predictions = []
for i in range(test_size):
    train = series[:-(test_size-i)]
    model = arch_model(train, p=1)
    model_fit = model.fit(disp='off')
    pred = model_fit.forecast(horizon=1)
    rolling_predictions.append(np.sqrt(pred.variance.values[-1,:][0]))

plt.figure(figsize=(10,4))
true, = plt.plot(vols[-test_size:])
preds, = plt.plot(rolling_predictions)
plt.title('Volatility Prediction')
plt.legend(['True Volatility', 'Predicted Volatility'])
